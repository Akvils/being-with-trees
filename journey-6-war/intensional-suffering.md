#### Fasting as a Spiritual Practice

Fasting, silence, heat, cold, prayer, meditation, and long walks in nature have been used for centuries as spiritual practices across many traditions. Their purpose is not simply discipline or endurance, but awakening.

In ordinary life, we are often driven by automatic impulses: hunger, comfort-seeking, fear, distraction, and the constant chatter of the mind. The moment discomfort appears, the mind says, “I need to escape this.” But spiritual practice invites us to pause and remain present.

When we fast, the first experience is usually resistance: “I’m hungry. I want food.” But if we stay conscious and present with the sensation, something begins to change. We discover that hunger is a passing experience, not our entire identity. Beyond the initial struggle, there can arise a deeper clarity, stillness, and connection - a sense that awareness itself is larger than the body’s immediate demands.

The same principle appears in practices like sauna, cold plunge, meditation, or a long hike through nature. At first the mind complains: “It’s too hot. It’s too cold. I’m tired.” But when we stop fighting the experience and meet it fully, the resistance softens. Presence deepens. The noise of the ego quiets, and a more spacious awareness emerges.

This is why many spiritual traditions use voluntary simplicity and discomfort as tools for transformation. They are not worshipping suffering. Rather, they are training the heart and mind to become free from automatic reactivity. In that freedom, many people experience a profound sense of peace, clarity, and connection with the Divine.

Fasting, then, is not merely abstaining from food. It is a practice of remembering what is deeper than appetite — a way of turning inward, becoming present, and opening oneself to God.
